Based on: “SpeedSim: Made to Exit!” by Naeem Zafar & Victoria Chang

The Core Dilemma

What if you launched a startup not to change the world, but to exit—fast and well? This case explores the disciplined, frugal, and laser-focused strategy of building a business with the primary goal of being acquired.

What Would You Do?

You're Kevin Ladd. You have a profitable, high-demand product. Quickturn has returned to the table after their other deal failed. Your leverage is at its peak. What's your move?

✅ What They Did & The Takeaway

The founders played their hand perfectly. They understood their window was short but their leverage was high. They rejected the early offer, used a competitor's failed deal to create scarcity, and maintained extreme financial discipline. They sold at their peak moment. While the post-acquisition integration failed, they achieved their primary mission: a massively successful exit.

Align from Day One: Agree with co-founders whether you’re building to sell, scale, or sustain.
Stay Lean: Avoid raising money that creates pressure to grow unsustainably.
Own Your Runway: Maximize control and negotiation leverage by controlling your burn.
Play the VC Game Wisely: Create competition. Don’t let VCs control the board.
Know Your Buyer: Build features, team, and clients that match your ideal acquirer’s wishlist.

The Core Dilemma

You're deep in debt. A final race could mean survival, but an engine failure on TV could mean bankruptcy. With ambiguous data on failure risk in cold weather, do you risk it all?

What Would You Do?

You're BJ Carter. The data is ambiguous, your team is divided, and time is running out. Do you trust the incomplete data and gut feelings, or do you pull out?

✅ What They Did & The Takeaway

This case is a simulation. The key insight emerges from data analysis: all seven engine failures occurred at the lowest race-day temperatures. The team's critical mistake was analyzing only the failures, not the successes, leading to a flawed risk assessment. They suffered from survivorship bias in reverse.

Beware of Incomplete Data: Never make a decision on a biased sample. Actively seek the data you don't have.
Structure Your Decisions: High pressure plus emotion equals bad choices. A framework brings clarity.
Encourage Dissent: Create psychological safety for team members to challenge the consensus.
Define Risk Boundaries: Know what level of failure you can afford—and what level will sink you.
Slow Down to Speed Up: A one-hour pause to analyze properly can prevent a year-long disaster.

📘 Culture as Strategy

Based on: “Roche Pakistan (B)” by Prof. Jennifer Chatman & Victoria Chang

The Core Dilemma

Can a deliberate shift in company culture directly drive a dramatic business turnaround in just 12 months, or is "culture" just a buzzword for consultants?

What Would You Do?

You're Farrukh Rehan. The business is back on track. The culture shift is palpable but fragile. Do you double down on aggressive growth, or slow down to ensure the cultural changes are deeply embedded?

✅ What They Did & The Takeaway

Rehan's team focused on embedding the new culture into the company's core systems. They made it central to leadership development and recruitment, used cross-functional teams to accelerate launches, and improved employee support. They balanced empathy with performance, proving that culture is not just a soft metric but a hard driver of results.

Link Culture to Strategy: Define cultural traits that directly support your business goals.
Put Values into Structure: Reinforce culture via teams, rituals, and compensation.
Make Leadership Visible: Leaders must actively model the desired behaviors.
Prioritize Internal Successors: Don't let transformation hinge on a single leader.
Balance Empathy with Performance: Culture without accountability leads to comfort, not growth.

Actionable Strategic Frameworks

This final section synthesizes the key lessons from all case studies into a library of strategic moves. Use these frameworks to guide your decision-making in startup strategy, data analysis, and cultural leadership.

🚀 Exit Strategy & Fundraising

  • Align founders on the end goal from day one.
  • Stay lean to maximize control and leverage.
  • Own your financial runway; don't chase bad money.
  • Create competition among potential investors.
  • Build what your ideal acquirer wants to buy.

📊 Data-Driven Decisions

  • Beware of incomplete data; seek what's missing.
  • Use structured frameworks under pressure.
  • Actively encourage and listen to dissenting voices.
  • Clearly define your acceptable risk boundaries.
  • Slow down to analyze; speed up execution.

🤝 Culture & Leadership

  • Link cultural initiatives directly to business strategy.
  • Embed values into organizational structure.
  • Ensure leadership visibly models the desired culture.
  • Build a pipeline of internal succession.
  • Balance empathy with performance and accountability.
